Hire Fast or Hire Right? A Contractor's Guide to Driver Screening

The pressure to fill a route fast is real — but rushing a bad hire costs you far more than an empty seat. Here's how to move quickly without cutting corners.

When a route goes uncovered, the pressure to fill it immediately is intense. Operations suffer, service scores drop, and you end up asking experienced drivers to stretch themselves thin. So it makes sense that most contractors default to hiring whoever shows up first.

But that instinct is expensive. A driver who clears the speed round only to fail on day three — because of an undisclosed MVR issue, a prior termination pattern, or simply not being suited for the role — costs you more than a week of an open seat. You've spent time onboarding, possibly equipment, and now you're starting over under even more pressure.

The real answer isn't to choose between fast and right. It's to build a process that's both.

What You Actually Need to Screen

Not every step in a typical hiring checklist is equally valuable. Here's what matters for last-mile driver roles:

  • Motor Vehicle Record (MVR): Non-negotiable. Pull it early. A clean MVR is the single best predictor of on-route safety and insurability.
  • Background Check: Required for DOT-regulated positions. Use an FCRA-compliant provider and define your adjudication criteria before you start — not after the report comes back.
  • Drug Test: Pre-employment drug screening is required under DOT rules. Build a relationship with a local clinic that can turn results in 24–48 hours.
  • Employment Verification: Two to three prior employers is sufficient. You're looking for patterns — not a full career audit. Flag unexplained gaps and short tenures.
  • Structured Interview: A focused 15-minute call beats a lengthy in-person process for volume roles. Cover reliability, availability, commute, and prior driving experience.

What You Can Skip or Streamline

You don't need a full reference check from every employer. You don't need a personality assessment. You don't need a multi-day interview process for a delivery driver role.

What slows most contractors down isn't thoroughness — it's unclear criteria and poor vendor relationships. Know your go/no-go list before a candidate enters the pipeline and you can make decisions in hours, not days.

A written adjudication matrix takes 30 minutes to create and saves you hours of deliberation on every hire. List the specific violations, patterns, or history that disqualify a candidate outright, and those that require a closer look. When the report comes back, you already know the answer.

Building a Process That Actually Moves

The contractors who hire well and hire fast share three habits:

  1. Vendors are set up before they're needed. Background check account, drug testing clinic, MVR access — all established in advance, not scrambled for during a shortage.
  2. Steps run in parallel. The MVR pull and application review happen simultaneously. The background check triggers the same day as the verbal offer.
  3. Decision criteria are written down. No deliberating. No "let me think about it." The process makes the decision; the hiring manager confirms it.

With this structure in place, a qualified candidate can move from application to offer in 48 to 72 hours. That's fast enough to respond to real operational needs without gambling on an unvetted hire.

The Bottom Line

Hiring fast and hiring right aren't opposites. The contractors who struggle are the ones who haven't built the infrastructure to do both. Invest 30 minutes now setting up your screening process — and it'll pay you back every single time you need to fill a seat under pressure.

If you're spending more than a week going from application to start date, the bottleneck isn't the candidate. It's the process.
