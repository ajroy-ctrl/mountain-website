How to Build a Driver Hiring Plan Before Peak Season Hits

Peak season doesn't wait for you to get ready — and neither does the driver shortage. Here's how to build a 90-day hiring plan that keeps your routes covered when volume surges.

Every year, the same story plays out across the last-mile industry: volume spikes, routes multiply, and operators scramble to find drivers who were already placed months ago. The contractors who stay covered aren't lucky. They planned ahead.

Why Peak Season Breaks Pipelines

Peak season doesn't just increase your headcount need — it compresses your lead time. The drivers you're competing for in October are the same ones every other carrier and contractor in your market wants. Job boards flood with postings, pay rates climb, and response rates fall. By the time you're actively recruiting, the best candidates have already committed elsewhere.

The solution isn't to recruit harder in October. It's to recruit earlier — when the competition is thinner and candidates have fewer options in front of them.

The 90-Day Rule

If you need drivers on routes by November 1st, your sourcing effort should start by August 1st. Here's the real timeline:

  • Weeks 1–2: Job posting goes live, applications start arriving. Volume is low at first — expect 5–10 serious applicants per week.
  • Weeks 3–4: Screening, MVR pulls, background checks. This takes time even with a fast vendor. Budget 5–7 business days per candidate.
  • Weeks 5–6: Offers extended, drug tests ordered, onboarding paperwork completed.
  • Weeks 7–8: Training period. New drivers need route shadowing and orientation before they run independently.
  • Weeks 9–10: Buffer for dropoffs, no-shows, and early turnover. Plan for 15–20% attrition before day 30.

That's a ten-week process under normal conditions. In a competitive fall market, add two more weeks.

Building Your Actual Headcount Number

Start with route projections. How many routes are you running today? What does your contract say about Q4 volume? Add a 15% coverage buffer for PTO, callouts, and unexpected absences.

From that number, work backwards. If you need 12 drivers operational by peak and expect 20% fallout through the hiring process, you need to start 15 people through onboarding. That means screening 50–60 applicants, which means generating 200+ impressions on your job posting. Know those numbers before you start — not after you realize you're short.

The Sourcing Channels That Actually Work

Paid job boards get all the attention, but the highest-yield sources for last-mile drivers are often free or low-cost:

  • Driver referrals from your existing team. Offer a $200–$300 bonus paid after 60 days. Drivers refer people they'd want to work alongside.
  • Local CDL training programs. Recent graduates are motivated, coachable, and not yet locked into another offer.
  • Community Facebook groups for drivers in your area. A genuine post from an operator gets far more engagement than a sponsored listing.
  • Re-engagement of prior applicants who didn't accept or didn't complete onboarding. They already know your operation.

What a Good Hiring Plan Looks Like

A one-page document with five things: target start dates and headcount by date, sourcing channels and budget per channel, screening timeline and vendor contacts, decision criteria for MVR and background, and a backup plan if hiring falls behind pace.

Review it monthly — not just at peak. The contractors who stay ahead of driver demand treat recruiting like an operational function, not an emergency response.

Don't wait until you're short-staffed.
