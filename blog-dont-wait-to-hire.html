Why Most Contractors Wait Too Long to Hire (And How to Get Ahead of It)

Reactive hiring costs more than proactive recruiting ever will. Here's how to spot the warning signs early and build a pipeline that runs before you need it.

The most common recruiting mistake in last-mile operations isn't a bad job posting or a slow screening process. It's timing. Most contractors don't start recruiting until they're already short-staffed — and by then, every decision they make is more expensive, more rushed, and more likely to go wrong.

Reactive hiring has a compounding cost. When you're scrambling, you accept candidates you'd otherwise pass on. You skip steps in the screening process. You onboard people before they're fully ready. And then, when those hires don't work out, you're back at the start — except now you're even more behind.

The Warning Signs You're Already Behind

Most contractors are already in reactive mode before they realize it. Watch for these signals:

  • Your existing drivers are regularly running overtime to cover open routes — a sign you're running below your coverage factor.
  • You've had uncovered routes in the past 60 days, even briefly.
  • You have a driver who has given informal signals they're thinking of leaving — and no one ready to replace them.
  • You're approaching a volume increase (seasonal, contract expansion) with no pipeline in progress.
  • Your last hire took more than three weeks from posting to start date.

If two or more of these apply to you right now, you're already in a gap you may not feel yet.

The Lead Time Problem

Here's the math that surprises most operators: from the moment you post a job to the day a driver runs their first independent route, you're typically looking at four to six weeks. That timeline includes sourcing, screening, background checks, onboarding paperwork, drug testing, and route training.

That means if you post today because you're short, your new driver won't be fully operational for a month and a half. Whatever gap you're managing right now has to be managed manually — with overtime, split routes, or borrowed help — for the entire time your new hire is coming up to speed.

If you had started recruiting six weeks before you felt the gap, it wouldn't have been a gap at all.

What a Rolling Pipeline Looks Like

Proactive contractors don't recruit only when they need drivers. They maintain a light, continuous sourcing effort at all times — even when fully staffed. The goal isn't to hire someone every week. It's to always have two or three qualified candidates who are warm, pre-screened, and ready to move quickly when an opening appears.

In practice, this means keeping a job posting live on a rotation, building a database of prior applicants, and running one or two sourcing touchpoints per month even in stable periods. The cost is minimal. The payoff — when a driver leaves unexpectedly — is enormous.

How to Start Today

  1. Audit your current coverage ratio. If you're below 1.15 drivers per route, start recruiting now.
  2. Identify your highest flight-risk drivers. Who has been with you less than 90 days? Who has missed shifts recently? Plan for their departure before it happens.
  3. Keep one job posting live at all times. Even if you're not actively hiring, a live posting keeps warm candidates in your funnel.
  4. Build a "ready bench" of 2–3 pre-screened candidates. People who've cleared your initial screens and expressed interest. Touch base with them monthly.

Proactive recruiting doesn't require a lot of effort. It requires consistency. And that consistency is the difference between an operation that stays covered and one that's always catching up.
