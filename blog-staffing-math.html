The Staffing Math Every Last-Mile Carrier Needs to Know

Most logistics service providers set headcount by feel — and end up shorthanded when it counts most. Here's the formula that actually works.

Ask most service providers how they determine their driver headcount and you'll hear some version of "we figure it out as we go." That approach works fine until it doesn't — and when it fails, it fails at the worst possible time: peak season, high-volume weeks, or when two drivers call out on the same morning.

Getting staffing right isn't complicated, but it does require working from numbers rather than instinct. Here's how to do it.

Start With Your Route Count, Not Your Driver Count

The first mistake most operators make is thinking in terms of how many drivers they have rather than how many routes they need covered. Routes are the unit of output. Drivers are the resource that covers them. Build your math around the routes.

How many routes does your contract require on a typical day? What's the peak-day volume? What does your contract allow for volume growth over the next 12 months? Answer those questions first.

The Coverage Factor

No driver works every single day. Vacations, sick days, training, and turnover all create gaps. The coverage factor accounts for this — it's the multiplier you apply to your route count to get your true headcount need.

Coverage Factor Formula Routes × Coverage Factor = Drivers Needed

For most last-mile operations, a coverage factor of 1.15 to 1.25 is appropriate. That means if you run 20 routes, you need 23–25 drivers on payroll to maintain consistent coverage.

If you're running lean — say, 1.05 — one or two callouts can cascade into uncovered routes fast. If you're running heavy — above 1.3 — you're carrying unnecessary payroll cost. The right number for your operation depends on your absenteeism rate, average tenure, and seasonal volume swings.

Driver-to-Route Ratios in Practice

A 1:1 driver-to-route ratio sounds obvious, but it's a trap. That ratio assumes perfect attendance and zero turnover, which never happens in last-mile operations. The practical minimum is 1.15:1. The practical target for a stable operation with consistent service scores is 1.2:1.

If you're running below 1.1:1 for more than a few weeks, you're in a fragile position. A single bad week — illness spreading through the team, an unexpected termination, a no-show — can leave routes uncovered with no buffer.

How Stop Volume Growth Inflates Your Headcount Need

Many service providers calculate their headcount once and revisit it annually. But stop volume grows continuously, and with it, your effective route burden per driver. A driver who was covering 120 stops per day two years ago may now be running 145. That's a different job — and eventually, a different headcount requirement.

Track your stops-per-route trend quarterly. When average daily stops exceed your target threshold, that's the signal to either add routes, add drivers, or renegotiate your contract — before performance suffers.

Putting It Together

Full Headcount Formula Step 1: Determine daily route count (including peak projections)
Step 2: Multiply by your coverage factor (1.15–1.25)
Step 3: Add 1–2 drivers as a training pipeline buffer
Step 4: Revisit quarterly as stop volume changes

Run this math today against your current headcount. If the number you get is higher than the drivers you have on payroll, you already have a gap — you just haven't felt it yet.
